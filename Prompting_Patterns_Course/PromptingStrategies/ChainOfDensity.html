Pattern: Chain of Density

Chain of Density: Mastering the Art of Summarization

Summarization is one of the most common tasks for LLMs, but creating a "good" summary is surprisingly difficult. A summary that is too short misses key details, while one that is too long isn't really a summary. The "Chain of Density" (CoD) pattern is a specialized prompting technique designed to create summaries that are increasingly "dense" with information while maintaining a fixed length.

The Problem: 'Vague' vs. 'Dense' Summaries

Standard summarization prompts often result in summaries that are "fluffy"â€”they use many words to convey very few actual facts. A "dense" summary, on the other hand, packs as much unique information as possible into a small number of words.

How Chain of Density Works

CoD uses an iterative, multi-step process within a single prompt. Typically, it involves 5 rounds of refinement:

  1. Round 1: The model writes an initial, "sparse" summary (e.g., 80 words) and identifies 1-3 "Missing Entities" (key facts, names, or concepts) from the original text that weren't in the summary.
  2. Round 2: The model rewrites the summary, keeping the length the same but "fusing" in the missing entities. It then identifies a new set of missing entities.
  3. Round 3-5: The process repeats. Each time, the summary becomes more "dense" as more unique information is packed into the same word count.

The Resulting Output

By the 5th round, you get a summary that is incredibly information-rich. Every word is working hard. Research shows that humans often prefer these "denser" summaries (up to a certain point) because they provide a much better overview of the source material for the same reading effort.

Implementing CoD in a Prompt

The system prompt for CoD is quite specific. It must define what an "entity" is and provide clear instructions for the iterative process. Example:

"I will provide you with an article. You will generate 5 increasingly dense versions of an 80-word summary. For each version: 1. Identify 1-3 'Missing Entities' from the article. 2. Rewrite the summary to include these entities while staying under 80 words. 3. Ensure the new summary is more informative but still easy to read. Format your response as a list of 5 summaries, each with its list of 'Missing Entities'."

Why CoD Works

  • Forceful Focus: By keeping the word count fixed, the model is forced to remove "fluff" and "filler" words to make room for new, important facts.
  • Iterative Discovery: The process of identifying "missing entities" helps the model consciously explore the source text for more detail.
  • Semantic Compression: The model learns to use more sophisticated language and sentence structures to combine multiple ideas into one.

Use Cases for Chain of Density

  • Executive Summaries: Providing a high-level overview of a long report where every word counts.
  • News Aggregators: Creating "dense" snippets of news stories for mobile apps or newsletters.
  • Academic Abstract Generation: Summarizing complex research papers into a concise but comprehensive abstract.
  • Social Media Content: Crafting high-impact tweets or LinkedIn posts from longer articles.

Practical Exercise: Densifying a Technical Blog Post

Take a 1,000-word blog post about a new technology. Run the Chain of Density prompt on it. Compare the "Round 1" summary to the "Round 5" summary. Which one gives you a better understanding of the technology? At which round does the summary become "too dense" or hard to read?

Limitations

CoD is very demanding for the model. It requires high reasoning capabilities and the ability to strictly follow length constraints. It works best with highly capable models like Claude 3.5 Sonnet. Furthermore, after 5 or 6 rounds, the summary can become "over-dense," losing its narrative flow and becoming just a list of facts.

In conclusion, the Chain of Density pattern is a powerful tool for anyone who needs to convey maximum information with minimum words. It turns summarization from a simple "shrunk-down" version of the original into a sophisticated exercise in semantic compression.
