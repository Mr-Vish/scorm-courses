AI Governance Frameworks and Standards

Why AI Governance Matters

Generative AI introduces unique risks that traditional IT governance doesn't address: hallucinations, bias amplification, data leakage, and unpredictable outputs. Organizations need structured frameworks to deploy AI responsibly while maintaining innovation velocity.

Real-World Impact: In 2023, Samsung banned ChatGPT after engineers accidentally leaked proprietary code. Air Canada's chatbot provided incorrect refund information, and the airline was held legally liable. These incidents highlight why governance isn't optional.

NIST AI Risk Management Framework

The NIST AI RMF provides a structured approach to managing AI risks across four functions:

# NIST AI RMF Core Functions

1. GOVERN
   - Establish AI governance structure and policies
   - Define roles, responsibilities, and accountability
   - Create risk appetite and tolerance levels
   
2. MAP
   - Identify AI use cases and stakeholders
   - Document context, purpose, and intended use
   - Assess potential impacts and risks
   
3. MEASURE
   - Test for accuracy, bias, and robustness
   - Monitor performance and drift
   - Evaluate against defined metrics
   
4. MANAGE
   - Implement controls and mitigations
   - Document decisions and trade-offs
   - Respond to incidents and update systems

ISO/IEC 42001 Management System

ISO 42001 defines requirements for establishing, implementing, and maintaining an AI management system:

  • Context: Understand organizational needs, stakeholders, and external factors
  • Leadership: Top management commitment, policy, roles and responsibilities
  • Planning: Risk assessment, objectives, and planning to achieve them
  • Support: Resources, competence, awareness, communication, documentation
  • Operation: Operational planning, AI system lifecycle management
  • Performance: Monitoring, measurement, analysis, evaluation, internal audit
  • Improvement: Nonconformity, corrective action, continual improvement

Governance Maturity Levels

Level 1: Ad Hoc
- No formal governance
- Individual teams make decisions
- Reactive to incidents

Level 2: Developing
- Basic policies documented
- Some review processes
- Inconsistent enforcement

Level 3: Defined
- Comprehensive policies
- Clear roles and processes
- Regular reviews and audits

Level 4: Managed
- Quantitative metrics tracked
- Proactive risk management
- Continuous improvement

Level 5: Optimizing
- Industry-leading practices
- Predictive risk management
- Innovation with guardrails
