EU AI Act Deep Dive

The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. Understanding its requirements is essential for global operations:

Risk-Based Classification

# EU AI Act Risk Tiers

UNACCEPTABLE RISK (Prohibited)
- Social scoring by governments
- Real-time biometric identification in public spaces (with exceptions)
- Exploitation of vulnerabilities (age, disability)
- Subliminal manipulation causing harm

HIGH-RISK AI SYSTEMS
Categories:
1. Biometric identification and categorization
2. Critical infrastructure management
3. Education and vocational training (admissions, assessment)
4. Employment (recruitment, promotion, termination)
5. Essential services (credit scoring, emergency dispatch)
6. Law enforcement (evidence evaluation, risk assessment)
7. Migration and border control
8. Justice and democratic processes

Requirements for High-Risk Systems:
✓ Risk management system
✓ Data governance and quality
✓ Technical documentation
✓ Record-keeping and logging
✓ Transparency and user information
✓ Human oversight
✓ Accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity
✓ Conformity assessment before deployment
✓ Registration in EU database

LIMITED RISK (Transparency Obligations)
- Chatbots: Disclose AI interaction
- Emotion recognition: Inform users
- Biometric categorization: Inform users
- Deepfakes: Label as AI-generated

MINIMAL RISK (No Obligations)
- AI-enabled games, spam filters
- Voluntary codes of conduct encouraged

GDPR Article 22: Automated Decision-Making

# GDPR Article 22 Requirements

Right Not to Be Subject to Automated Decision-Making:
- Individuals have the right not to be subject to decisions based 
  solely on automated processing that produce legal or similarly 
  significant effects

Exceptions (Automated Decisions Allowed If):
1. Necessary for contract performance
2. Authorized by EU or member state law
3. Based on explicit consent

Additional Safeguards Required:
✓ Right to obtain human intervention
✓ Right to express point of view
✓ Right to contest the decision
✓ Right to obtain an explanation

Implementation for AI Systems:
- Classify decisions by significance (legal, financial, etc.)
- Implement human-in-the-loop for significant decisions
- Provide explanation mechanisms (SHAP, LIME, etc.)
- Document decision logic and factors
- Enable appeals process

Compliance Mapping Process

# Regulatory Compliance Mapping

Step 1: Identify Applicable Regulations
- Geography: Where is the AI deployed and where are users located?
- Industry: Healthcare, finance, education, etc.
- Data Types: Personal data, health data, financial data, biometric
- Use Case: Employment, credit, law enforcement, etc.

Step 2: Map Requirements to AI System
For each regulation:
- List specific requirements
- Determine applicability to the AI system
- Identify gaps between current state and requirements
- Assess compliance risk (high/medium/low)

Step 3: Develop Compliance Plan
- Prioritize high-risk gaps
- Assign owners and deadlines
- Implement technical and process controls
- Document compliance evidence

Step 4: Ongoing Monitoring
- Track regulatory changes
- Update compliance mapping quarterly
- Conduct compliance audits
- Report to governance board

Example Mapping:
AI System: Customer Credit Scoring
Regulations: ECOA, FCRA, GDPR (if EU customers)

ECOA Requirements:
✓ No discrimination on protected characteristics
✓ Adverse action notices with reasons
✓ Monitoring for disparate impact
Gap: Need to implement explainability for adverse actions
Owner: Model Owner | Due: 60 days | Risk: High

FCRA Requirements:
✓ Accuracy of data
✓ Dispute resolution process
✓ Reasonable procedures to ensure accuracy
Gap: Dispute resolution process not documented
Owner: Compliance Officer | Due: 30 days | Risk: Medium

Compliance Documentation

Maintain comprehensive records to demonstrate compliance:

  • Records of Processing Activities (ROPA): GDPR Article 30 requires documentation of all processing activities
  • Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIA): Required for high-risk processing under GDPR
  • Legitimate Interest Assessments (LIA): If relying on legitimate interest as lawful basis
  • Consent Records: If relying on consent, maintain records of when and how obtained
  • Data Transfer Mechanisms: Standard contractual clauses, binding corporate rules
  • Vendor Assessments: Due diligence on third-party AI providers
  • Training Records: Evidence of employee training on compliance
  • Incident Logs: Breaches, near-misses, and responses

Preparing for Regulatory Inquiries

# Regulatory Inquiry Response Plan

When Regulator Contacts Organization:
1. Acknowledge receipt within 24 hours
2. Assemble response team (Legal, Compliance, AI Governance, Model Owner)
3. Preserve all relevant documents and data
4. Do not delete or modify AI systems or logs

Information Typically Requested:
- AI system inventory and descriptions
- Risk assessments and approvals
- Model cards and technical documentation
- Training data sources and characteristics
- Bias testing and fairness audits
- Incident reports and remediation
- Policies and procedures
- Training records

Response Timeline:
- Initial response: 5-10 business days
- Full documentation: 30-60 days (varies by regulator)
- Follow-up questions: 10-15 business days

Best Practices:
✓ Maintain organized documentation proactively
✓ Conduct mock regulatory inquiries annually
✓ Designate regulatory liaison
✓ Legal review before submitting responses
✓ Track all communications with regulator
