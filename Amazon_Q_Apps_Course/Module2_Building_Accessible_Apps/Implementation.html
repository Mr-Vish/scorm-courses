Practical Implementation: Case Studies in Accessibility

Case Study 1: The "Meeting Inclusive Summary" App

In many enterprises, employees with hearing impairments or those who process auditory information differently struggle with long, unformatted meeting transcripts. Using Amazon Q Apps, a team built a "Meeting Inclusive Summary" tool.

Implementation Details:

  • Input Card: Users upload the meeting transcript (text file).
  • Query Card: The AI extracts key action items and decisions, but specifically looks for "social context"â€”who said what and the emotional tone of the meeting.
  • Output Card: The summary is generated in a high-contrast format with clear "Next Steps" and a glossary of any technical terms used during the call.

Accessibility Impact: This app reduced the time required to review meetings by 60% for neurodivergent employees and provided a critical alternative for those who could not attend the live session due to sensory overload.

Case Study 2: HR Policy Simplified

Enterprise HR policies are often written in dense, legalistic language that is difficult for many users to navigate. An HR department used Amazon Q Apps to build an "Easy-Read Policy Guide."

Implementation Details:

  • Data Source: The app was connected to the official enterprise HR handbook via Amazon Q Business.
  • Selection Card: Users select a topic (e.g., "Parental Leave," "Remote Work Policy").
  • Query Card: The system prompt instructs: "Explain this policy as if to a person who is new to the company. Use short sentences and no jargon."

Accessibility Impact: This lowered the barrier for employees with learning disabilities and non-native speakers, ensuring they could access their benefits and rights without confusion.

Advanced Implementation: Context Injection for Assistive Tech

For advanced builders, "Context Injection" involves providing the AI with information about the user's preferred accessibility settings. While Amazon Q Apps is still evolving, builders can currently implement this by adding a "Preferences" input card.

Pro Tip: By including a card where users can specify their needs (e.g., "I prefer bullet points" or "I need high-contrast descriptions"), you can pass this information as a variable to all subsequent Query cards, ensuring the entire app experience is personalized for the user's specific accessibility requirements.

Testing Your Q App for Accessibility

Before sharing your app with the organization, perform these three simple tests:

  1. The Keyboard Test: Can you navigate every part of the app using only the 'Tab' and 'Enter' keys?
  2. The Screen Reader Test: Turn on a screen reader (VoiceOver on Mac, Narrator on Windows) and listen to how the app is described. Are the labels clear?
  3. The "Simple Language" Test: Ask a colleague who is unfamiliar with the topic to read the AI's output. Do they understand it immediately?

Key Takeaways

  • Real-world apps like meeting summarizers and policy simplifiers provide immediate accessibility value.
  • Personalizing outputs based on user preferences is a powerful way to enhance inclusivity.
  • Testing with keyboards and screen readers is a mandatory step in the development process.
